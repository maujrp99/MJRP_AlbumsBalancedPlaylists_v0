So, Im deciding to revisit this album because I think I can make a better review and also just to see if this album will grow on me more since I gave it an 8. But anyway, now that Ive listened to this album all the way through, I can safely say that this has actually become one of my favorite albums from the band. The production is fantastic and the more mainstream sound they went for on this album I think really paid off and James Hetfields performance throughout was fantastic. The Unforgiven is a song I actually had to listen to twice since when I first heard it, I thought it was good, but didnt consider it one of my favorites, but now I appreciate it much more. The acoustic guitar parts are beautiful and amazing and James singing on the chorus is just beautiful and amazing. Plus, the riffs on this one are great and the guitar solos on this song are fantastic too. Wherever I May Roam is one of the longer songs on here with it being 6 minutes and 45 seconds in length and its a great song. The riffs and solos are great on this one and James performance on this song are great as well. Dont Tread On Me is a song that I considered to be my least favorite on here and listening to it again, its a good song and I like some parts about it, but compared to the others, its one of the weaker songs for me. Through The Never is one of my personal favorites on here and I just love the energy this song brings with these great and catchy riffs and solos and James vocals on this one are just badass. Nothing Else Matters is the bands biggest song theyve ever put out and its one of the best and most beautiful metal songs. The best part of this song is James performance. I think he killed it on this song and they literally give me chills every time I hear it. Plus, the riffs on this song are incredible and amazing and that solo that comes later on the song is fantastic too. Of Wolf And Man is another song I didnt consider a favorite of mine and listening to it again, its actually grown on me to where I actually love it know. The riffs are pretty catchy and the solo on this one is insane. The God That Failed is another great song although I will admit it does get kinda repetitive. But, I cant deny how great the guitar solo is on this song too. Also, the bass at the beginning of the song is really sick. My Friend Of Misery is the longest song on here with it being 6 minutes and 50 seconds in length and its a fantastic song. Like, first off the bass on this song is fantastic especially that riff at the beginning of the song. So overall, this album has actually surpassed KEA and has become my 3rd favorite album from them. Pretty much every song on here is fantastic except for Dont Tread On Me, but even then that song is still good. So Keane if you see this, please listen to this album because I promise you, it is worth it and dont go in expecting it to be like RTL and MOP levels since the sound of this album is different.

The switch up

This album changed everything for Metallica. Before The Black Album, they were a pure thrash metal band, fast, aggressive, and always pushing their speed and technicality. But here, they took a different path. The songs became shorter, heavier, and more focused. Instead of long and intense tracks, Metallica went for simplicity and power. The result? One of the most successful and influential rock albums of all time. (Although I still prefer their older work) The faster rhythms of their 80s albums are replaced with slower, groovier, and more controlled playing. I assume not EVERYONE liked the change at the time, but it's hard to deny how strong this record is. I noticed Lars Ulrichs drumming is also different. Instead of wild and fast, he sticks to a more straightforward, pounding style. Some people like to hate on his playing, but on this album, I really do think his choices make sense. The slower tempos allow the drums to have more impact, giving the songs a sense of weight and force that suits the new direction. Then theres the vocals(I forgot the guy's name). It's still him, but it seems more confident and controlled. Hes not just shouting over fast riffs anymore, hes actually 'singing'. The melodies are more defined, the hooks are stronger, and the emotion in his voice comes through more clearly. Another BIG change is in the songwriting. The band moved away from the long song structures and instead focused on memorable choruses, strong hooks, and a more straightforward sound. I don't really think there is any denying the impact of The Black Album. So, is it a masterpiece? Almost. Its a brilliant album, but not a perfect one. The shift away from thrash took away some of their wild energy, and a few tracks dont hit as hard as the best ones. But at the same time, this album gave us some of Metallicas most iconic moments, and it still holds up after all these years. A solid 8/10, a great album. Goated. I know I've been only praising this album, but don't think that I prefer this over their old work. Definitely not. Their older records are some of THE BEST albums I've ever heard.

In honor of its 30th anniversary, I thought it would be good to finally review this classic. The Black album receives too much hate for no reason. Its a great album and it features some of Metallicas best work. This album was a huge shift in style for the band so of course metal fans quickly began calling them sellouts. As much as I loved their thrash metal era that is considered classic Metallica, I dont think they could have kept it up forever. Put yourself in their shoes, after 4 albums that are very similar in style, Im sure anyone would want to try something new. The formula they used on the black album is much more simplified, but it is so damn effective and they took a big step in the right direction lyrically. This album is dark, its still heavy and it features some of the bands most interesting songs. There are so many iconic riffs on this album that still hold up today. This album has a lot to offer and Ive always been confused as to why it is so hated. Recommended Tracks: Sad But True, The Unforgiven, Wherever I May Roam, The God That Failed.

The last GREAT Metallica album
starstarstarstarstar
star

People calling Metallica sellouts after "The Black Album" were probably the same fans that in 1988 called them sellouts for releasing "One" with a music video (even if it was an uncompromisingly noncommercial one). They're also probably the same fans that in 1984 called Metallica sellouts for writing their first ballad (even if that ballad happened to be Fade to Black). In short, change angers certain people, as does commercial success, it's no wonder the Black Album, Metallica's first giant leap away from Thrash AS WELL AS the biggest-selling metal album of all time, was and is so divisive amongst metal fans. Metallica's self-titled is actually wildly brave, sacrificing years of momentum developing the Thrash genre in favour of slower, more streamlined songs. But regardless of context, this album contains so many bangers. "Enter Sandman" is arguably the greatest and most recognisable Metal Riff of all time, "Sad But True" is incredibly heavy and catchy all at once, "The Unforgiven" contains Kirk's best guitar solo and "Wherever I May Roam" shows Metallica experimenting with eery eastern melodies and production techniques. Not to mention deep cuts like "The God That Failed" which features some of James' best writing. Also, on the point of production, can we appreciate how heavy and full Metallica sounds here? The Drum sound is some of the best on record, you can hear the bass fine and the guitars are bright and weighty. Bob Rock, despite his background, produced Metallica's heaviest and most accessible record all at once, that's sort of genius. There are some weak points and surprisingly it's actually the faster, thrashier songs like "Struggle Within" (intro slaps though) or "Through the Never." Just my opinion but the cleaner, crisper production doesn't lend itself to these rougher cuts. Either way, I still regard this as one of Metallica's "Classic" albums, it catapulted Metallica and Metal in general into the mainstream permanently, finally concluding Metallica's trajectory of thrash innovation and repackaging the group as the 90's biggest rock band.
